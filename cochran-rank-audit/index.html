What this measures

When someone searches for a car dealer, Google shows a small map with three businesses on it. That block, the "map pack", is where the calls and the lot visits come from. This report answers one question: for each search, in each town, is Cochran Motor Company in that block?

Each search was localised to the town it names, so the Cartersville column reflects what a person in Cartersville sees, not a national average. Alongside each term is its real monthly search volume, so position can be weighed against how much demand it represents.

Google Maps rankings were deliberately not used. Maps orders results by how close you are standing to each business, which flatters a dealer inside his own town and does not match what a real searcher sees. Everything here comes from the search results page.

Stated plainly: the limits of this data

  • Rankings drift by about one position day to day. Any single number here is a snapshot. The patterns across 520 searches are the reliable part, not any one cell.
  • 52 of the 520 searches returned no map pack at all. Google does not show a map block for every phrase. Those are recorded as "no pack" rather than counted as a loss.
  • "Near me" searches cannot be measured town by town. They follow the phone, not the words. They were priced but left out of the grid rather than reported as a number that cannot be stood behind.
  • This was checked against hand measurement. The same Rome terms were captured by hand in a live browser on 16 July. Both methods agree on the finding that matters: Cochran sat outside the top three then and sits outside it now.

The headline

Across 520 searches in 26 markets, Cochran Motor Company appears in the map pack zero times. Not in Cartersville, not in Calhoun, not in Cedartown, and not in Rome.

This is consistent with the July check rather than a contradiction of it. That check placed Cochran fourth and fifth on four Rome terms. Only the top three make the pack, so fourth place means what this wider study now shows everywhere: just outside the door.

The important part is where the door is

Rome is the largest market in this study at 37,713 people, and the lot sits a mile from the middle of it. The pack there is being lost to three named competitors, not to distance. That is a fixable problem, and it is where the work starts.

What the numbers mean

1.The problem is Rome, not the region

The instinct after seeing 26 markets of zeros is to reach farther. The data says the opposite.

All 277 businesses holding these packs were resolved to their exact street addresses, and the distance from each one to the town whose pack it holds was measured. The middle value is 3.0 miles. In used car retail, the map pack is won by being close. In 17 of the 25 markets outside Rome, every dealer holding a pack is physically closer to that town than Cochran is. Cartersville, Dalton, Canton, Woodstock, Fort Payne and the rest are not winnable from North Broad Street, and no amount of website work changes that.

Rome is different. It is the largest market in the study, the lot is a mile from the center of it, and the pack is being lost to three named competitors rather than to geography.

2.Proof that the Rome pack is winnable

If distance decided the Rome pack, only Rome dealers would be in it. That is not what was found.

Cartersville Trucks, based 22 miles away, holds a Rome map pack slot on "used trucks for sale." A dealer in another county reaches into Rome. Cochran, a mile from the center, does not appear.

That single fact establishes that the Rome pack is decided by how well Google understands a business, not by how close it sits.

3.Review count is not the gate

The obvious explanation for losing to Easy Auto and Trinity is their review counts, both near 1,000. That explanation does not survive the data.

Sherold Salmon holds 15 of the 19 Rome packs with 145 reviews, not a thousand. And SRI Auto Brokers holds two Rome slots with 34 reviews and a 3.9 star rating, a lower rating than Cochran's.

Reviews matter, and 21 is genuinely low. But businesses with 33, 34 and 126 reviews are in these blocks. Cochran holds the highest star rating in Rome and none of them. Something other than reputation is keeping him out, and that is the good news, because reputation is the slowest thing to change.

4.The largest single opportunity is buy here pay here

"Buy here pay here" is the highest volume search term in the entire 40 mile area at 1,190 searches a month, ahead of "used cars" at 1,060. Counting the related credit terms, the lane is worth 1,710 searches a month, and it produces a map pack in all 26 markets.

The dealership already does this work. The website says so in passing, but there is no page about it, and nothing on the Google Business Profile mentions it. Google has no way to know. The Rome pack for this term is currently held by Easy Auto, Trinity and SRI Auto Brokers, and SRI is holding its slot with 34 reviews.

5.The website is close to invisible

Cochranmotorco.com appears in the ordinary web results on 9 of 520 searches, all in Rome, at positions 9 through 18, which is the second page for most people.

The cause is structural. The current site assembles all of its content, including every vehicle, inside the visitor's browser after the page loads. Search engines receive a nearly empty page. Every individual vehicle page returns the same title and the same content as the home page, so there is nothing for Google to tell apart, index or rank. The same applies to the AI search tools a growing share of shoppers now use.

6.Where the profile itself is thin

The Google Business Profile is claimed, correctly addressed, has hours set and carries a 5.0 star rating. Two gaps stand out: three photographs, where dealers winning these packs carry dozens, and no secondary categories, so terms like truck dealer and the finance related searches have nothing to attach to.

7.The lot itself is under described

Of the 41 vehicles currently in inventory, 20 have no photographs at all, none have a written description, and 6 have no price. Half the lot is effectively invisible even to someone who finds the website. This is the least expensive problem in this report to fix, and it is not a website problem.

The plan

Where the effort goes, and where it does not

Rome first. It is the largest market in the study, the lot sits a mile from its center, and the pack there is lost on prominence rather than distance. After Rome: Lindale, Adairsville, Calhoun, Summerville, Cedartown and Rockmart, all markets where a dealer at Cochran's distance has been measured holding a pack.

Nothing gets spent on the far markets. Cartersville, Dalton, Canton, Woodstock, Acworth, Kennesaw, Fort Payne, Villa Rica and the other distant towns are held by dealers inside those towns. That was measured across 277 competitors rather than assumed. If it changes, the re measurement will show it.

What we are building

  1. Per vehicle pages on a rebuilt website Every vehicle gets its own page with its own title, photographs, description and specifications, in a form search engines and AI tools can read. Inventory keeps being managed in Wayne Reaves exactly as it is today. The new site reads that inventory automatically, so a vehicle entered in the morning has a page the same day with no extra work at the dealership. This turns 41 vehicles into 41 ways to be found, and it is what makes searches like "used 4x4 truck rome ga" reachable at all.
  2. A buy here pay here section The largest demand pocket in the market at 1,710 searches a month, currently unclaimed by this dealership despite being work it already does. This becomes a real part of the site and a matching set of services on the Google Business Profile.
  3. Google Business Profile build out Photographs, secondary categories that match what is actually sold, services, and a regular posting cadence. This is the only route to the 3,230 monthly "near me" searches, which no website page can capture.
  4. Review generation, automated Every sold vehicle produces an ask, without anyone at the dealership having to remember. Twenty one reviews is low enough to be the tiebreaker against businesses sitting at 145, even though the data shows reviews are not the gate.
  5. Tracking, from day one Calls and web enquiries get logged and attributed, so leads coming from this work are visible as they arrive rather than guessed at later.

What we need from the dealership

None of these take long, and the work above cannot fully land without them.

  • Photographs of the 20 vehicles that have noneHalf the lot cannot sell online without them, and no page or profile fixes that
  • Prices on the 6 vehicles missing oneUnpriced vehicles get skipped by shoppers and by Google
  • Access to the domain registrarTo point the new website live while the existing system keeps running alongside it
  • Manager access to the Google Business ProfileTo add photographs, categories, services and posts
  • A contact at Wayne ReavesOne request, to move the current site to its own address so the new site can take the main one
  • The list of past buyersThe starting point for the review programme

Continuing to enter vehicles in Wayne Reaves the same way as today, with photographs and a price, is the single most valuable ongoing habit. Everything downstream is built on that data.
